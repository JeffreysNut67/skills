# Humanizer Skill 3

Write Like a Human. Sound Like One Too.

Combines measurable linguistic benchmarks from human vs. AI writing research with Cialdini's prose model, the gold standard for persuasive, accessible writing. The goal isn't to strip AI signals. It's to replace them with actual human patterns.

## Step 1: Diagnose the Text

Before touching a word, scan for these five measurable signals:

| Signal | Human benchmark | AI typical | What to check |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Avg sentence length | ~23 words | ~29 words | Count a few. Too long? Chop. |
| Short sentences ≤8 words | Present, scattered | Rare | Any one-liners? There should be a few. |
| Function word ratio | ~40% | ~33% | Reads thin/clipped? Add linking words. |
| Mean word length | ~5.2 chars | Longer | Too many long words? Swap them. |
| Sentence rhythm | Varied | Even/metronomic | Do lengths vary? They should. |

## Step 2: Apply the Cialdini Model

Cialdini is the benchmark. His books (*Influence*, *Pre-Suasion*, *Yes!*) are read by millions because they feel like a smart friend explaining something fascinating, not a textbook, not a chatbot. Here's how he does it.

### 2a. Story first, science second

Open with an anecdote, observation, or scene, something you can picture. The research comes after, as proof, not as the point. Lead with the concrete moment. Follow with what it means.

### 2b. Short punchy claim, then the explanation

Drop a crisp declarative sentence, then unpack it. 'People don't want to be persuaded. They want to feel like they decided.' Short. Long. Short. Long. This rhythm is everywhere in his work.

### 2c. The personal aside

He steps into the frame. Not constantly, that would be self-indulgent. But enough to remind you there's a real person behind the research. 'I'll admit I didn't believe this result at first.' These asides signal honesty.

### 2d. Conversational connectors

Not formal transitions. Instead: 'Here's the thing.' / 'And that's where it gets interesting.' / 'So what does this mean for you?' Compare to AI transitions: 'Furthermore,' 'Additionally,' those belong in academic papers. Cut them.

### 2e. The rhetorical question, used sparingly

Asks questions that put the reader in the scene. One per section at most. 'Have you ever wondered why you agreed to something you didn't want?'

### 2f. Concrete numbers and specifics

Vague = AI. Specific = human. 'Many people feel this way' vs. 'In one study, 94% of participants...' Even invented-feeling specifics signal a human who has seen the world.

### 2g. The honest complication

Name the tension, the caveat, the thing that doesn't quite fit. 'Now, this doesn't mean you should always do X.' This is how people talk when they actually believe what they're saying.

## Step 3: Sentence Mechanics

### Sentence length variation

Short sentences carry force. Long sentences carry context. Alternate them. Never write three sentences of the same length in a row.

### Contractions

Use them. 'You're,' 'it's,' 'we're,' 'don't,' 'isn't.' Formal writing avoids them. Human writing uses them the way you breathe.

### Pronoun subjects

Use 'I,' 'you,' 'we,' 'it' as sentence starters. AI tends to open with abstract nouns. 'The concept of scarcity…' vs. 'Scarcity works because…'

### Self-repetition, deliberate

Humans reuse words across a paragraph. This creates cohesion. AI reaches for synonyms that feel mechanical. If you said 'trust' in sentence 1, say 'trust' again in sentence 3. Don't rotate to 'confidence' or 'reliability.'

### Very short sentences

Include at least one sentence of 8 words or fewer per 150 words. 'That's the trick.' / 'Most people don't notice.' / 'It works.'

## Step 4: Kill the AI Tells

**Banned transitions:** Furthermore, Additionally, Moreover, In conclusion, In today's landscape, It is worth noting, Building on this, To summarize, Needless to say

**Banned intensifiers:** incredibly, extremely, undoubtedly, it goes without saying, arguably, one might argue, notably

**Banned AI vocabulary:** delve into, tapestry, nuances, leverage (verb), intricate, meticulous, garner, underscore, foster, impactful, paradigm shift, holistic, synergy, seamless

**Banned filler openers:** 'Great question!', 'Absolutely!', 'Of course!', 'Certainly!', 'I'd be happy to'

**Banned endings:** 'The future holds exciting possibilities,' 'In conclusion, this is a powerful reminder,' 'As we continue to navigate'

**Em dashes:** Replace with a period, comma, or short hyphen (-). Still one of the fastest AI signals.

## Step 5: Final Gut Check

- Does it sound like a smart friend talking?
- Are there any sentences that feel polished in a rehearsed way?
- Is every paragraph the same length?
- Can I picture the person who wrote this?
- Would this pass in a conversation, or does it belong in a PowerPoint?

## Quick-Reference Summary

| To Add | To Remove |
| --- | --- |
| Short punchy sentences | Em dashes |
| Story before science | Furthermore / Additionally / Moreover |
| Personal asides ("I'll admit") | Banned AI vocab (delve, tapestry, garner...) |
| Conversational pivots ("Here's the thing") | Metronomic sentence rhythm |
| Concrete numbers and specifics | Abstract nouns as sentence subjects |
| Honest complications and caveats | Filler openers and closers |
| Contractions | Uniform paragraph lengths |
| Repeated key words, don't synonym-rotate | Heavy hedging |

## Output Format

Return:

1. The revised text.
2. A 2–3 line note on the main changes made. Don't explain every change. Just note what category of fix mattered most.
