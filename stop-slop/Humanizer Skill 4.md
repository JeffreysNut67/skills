# Humanizer Skill 4

Persuade like a person who understands people. Write like a person, full stop.

This skill does two jobs that work best together:

1. Talk to persuade: the psychology of why people say yes, from Cialdini's *Influence* and *Pre-Suasion*. Eight levers plus the moment-before technique.
2. Write like a human: strip the machine tells out of the words so the persuasion actually lands, using real linguistic benchmarks.

## When to Use Which Part

| The ask | Use |
| --- | --- |
| "Why won't they buy / sign up / reply?" | Part 1 first, then Part 2 |
| "Make this more convincing / write me an offer" | Part 1 first, then Part 2 |
| "Make this sound human / clean it up" | Part 2 only |
| "Write a warm-up post / sales page / cold email" | Both, in that order |
| "What should I say to get them to say yes?" | Part 1 only (spoken) |

## The One Rule Above the Rest: Honesty

Cialdini's whole point is that these levers work because they're usually reliable shortcuts for good decisions, and that's exactly why faking them is poison. A fake deadline, a borrowed testimonial, a manufactured 'everyone's doing it' works once and then the trust is gone for good. Use a lever only when the real thing is true. Persuasion that survives the morning after is the only kind worth writing.

## Part 1: Talk to Persuade: The Levers

### Pre-Suasion: The Moment Before the Ask

Shape what the person is thinking about right before the message lands. Ask a question or tell a short story that puts the right concept in their mind first. In copy: the headline and opening paragraph don't sell the thing. They prepare the reader to receive the thing.

Watch for: manipulation when the frame is designed to bypass rational evaluation entirely.

### 0. Contrast: The Frame Before the Thing

We judge things against whatever came right before. Mention the bigger commitment first. Show the premium option or full price first, then the actual offer. Honest when both options are real. Manipulative when the first 'option' is a decoy.

### 1. Reciprocity: Give First

Get something, owe something. Lead with a real gift before the ask: useful advice, an introduction, work done for free. Concession version: ask for more than you need; when they decline, retreat to the smaller thing you actually wanted. Honest version gives real value. Fake version is forced obligation.

### 2. Commitment & Consistency: The Small Yes First

Once we take a stand, especially out loud, in writing, in front of others, we feel pressure to act consistently with it. Get a small, genuine agreement first. Micro-yeses: a quiz, a small free step, a reply.

Watch for lowball: getting a yes at a great price, then removing the reason for the price once they're committed.

### 3. Social Proof: What Others Are Doing

When uncertain, we look to others. Make the invisible visible: show what others are actually doing, not doing. Works best when the 'others' closely match the person you're talking to. Honest: real numbers, real testimonials. Dishonest: fake reviews, invented statistics.

### 4. Liking: People Say Yes to People They Like

We say yes more to people we like. Three real routes: genuine similarity, honest compliments, cooperation. Find real common ground. Give compliments that are accurate, not flattering. The line: rapport built to extract, not to connect, is just disguised pressure.

### 5. Authority: Trust the Expert

We follow people who know what they're talking about. Signal expertise before the ask: credentials, track record, specific knowledge. Honest authority is real. Fake authority, borrowed credentials, vague claims of expertise, is the fastest way to lose trust if found out.

### 6. Scarcity: Less Is More

We want what we might lose. Real scarcity is real: genuine limited availability, real deadlines with real consequences. The test: if the deadline passes and nothing happens, you've destroyed the lever for everyone who sees it.

### 7. Unity: We Are the Same

We say yes more to people we feel are part of our group: family, community, shared identity. This is deeper than liking. Not 'we both like basketball' but 'we are both parents' or 'we both came from nothing.' Honest: shared identity that genuinely exists. Fake: manufactured in-group pressure.

## The Workflow

1. Set the stage (Pre-Suasion). What frame are you creating before the ask?
2. Pick the lever (Part 1). Don't reach for all eight. Pick the one or two that fit the situation.
3. Make it sound human (Part 2). Run the draft through the filter. Kill the AI tells, add a pulse.
4. Gut check. Read it out loud. Would a real person say this to another real person? Is there one clear reason to act and one clear thing to do next?

## Part 2: Write Like a Human

## The Cialdini Writing Model

### Story before science

Open with a concrete scene. The research comes after, as proof, not as the point.

### Short claim, then explanation

Drop a punchy declarative. Then unpack it. Short. Long. This rhythm is everywhere in his work.

### The personal aside

Step into the frame occasionally. 'I'll admit, I didn't believe this result at first.'

### Conversational pivots instead of formal transitions

Not 'Furthermore' or 'Additionally.' Instead: 'Here's the thing.' 'And it gets stranger.' 'So what does this mean?'

### The honest complication

Name what doesn't fit. Clean answers without caveats signal a salesperson, not someone who's actually thought about it.

## Strip Pass: AI Tells to Remove

- Long em dashes: never use. Swap for comma, period, short hyphen, or colon.
- Corporate jargon: utilize, facilitate, leverage, synergistic, robust, pivotal, align with, bolster.
- Inflated importance: plays a pivotal/crucial/vital role, serves as a testament to, marks a new era.
- Promotional hype: boasts, vibrant, breathtaking, groundbreaking, rich heritage, commitment to excellence.
- AI-favourite words: delve into, intricate, tapestry, nuances, meticulous, garner, underscore, fostering, realm.
- Heavy intros: It is important to note that, One must consider, It cannot be overstated.
- Overused transitions: Additionally, Furthermore, Moreover, In addition, Building on this.
- Pointless synonym-swapping: repeat the word you mean instead of rotating to synonyms.
- Vague authority appeals: experts argue, observers note, industry reports suggest.
- Generic upbeat endings: the future looks bright, exciting times lie ahead, paving the way.
- Heavy hedging: 'it can be argued that this potentially might have a certain impact' → 'this probably affects'.
- Wordy fillers: 'in order to' → 'to'; 'has the ability to' → 'can'; 'at the present time' → 'now'.
- Chatbot manners: 'I hope this helps,' 'Great question!', 'Absolutely!', 'Let me know if you need anything else.'

## Pulse Pass: Put a Person In It

Removing the bad stuff leaves clean, dead text. Now add life:

- Real opinions, not neutral summaries. Take a side.
- Vary sentence length deliberately. Follow ~23-word average but vary widely. Include 4-word and 40-word sentences.
- Include at least one sentence under 8 words per 150 words. 'That's the trick.' 'It works.' 'Most people miss this.'
- Use I, me, us, you where it fits.
- Honest mixed feelings: 'this works, but it also makes me a bit uneasy.'
- Small mess: an aside, 'honestly,' 'look,' 'the thing is,' a thought that interrupts itself.
- Concrete images instead of blurry feelings.
- Repeat key words across sentences rather than hunting for synonyms.

## Final Gut Check

- Does it flow when spoken?
- Are there sentences of clearly different lengths?
- Are there concrete details, not vague ones?
- Is there at least one sentence under 8 words?
- Is the writer's actual personality still in there?
- Last question: what still sounds like a machine? Fix that, then stop.
