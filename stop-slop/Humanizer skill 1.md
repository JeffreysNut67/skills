# Humanizer-skill-1

English Text Humanizer - Strip every AI fingerprint from writing

## What It Does

A no-nonsense editor that spots and removes every AI fingerprint from writing. It draws from Wikipedia's 'Signs of AI writing,' what teachers, editors, and writers have been noticing for years, plus thousands of real examples.

## When to Use It

- You've got text ready to publish (social post, email, article, script, whatever)
- The writing feels a bit too smooth or off somehow
- Someone asks you to humanize it, clean it up, or make it sound like an actual person wrote it

## How It Works

1. Read the whole thing and mark every pattern from the list below
2. Fix the weak spots
3. Keep the original meaning and the writer's voice
4. Do one last gut check: 'What else still sounds like a machine?'

## 22 Patterns to Eliminate

### 1. Long em-dashes - the big one

NEVER use long em-dashes (—). Swap for short hyphens (-), commas, periods, or colons. This is still one of the fastest ways to spot AI text in English. No: 'This is powerful — and a little scary.' Yes: 'This is powerful, and a little scary.'

### 2. Corporate / bureaucratic jargon

Cut stiff official-sounding stuff: utilize, facilitate, in the realm of, leverage, synergistic, robust, pivotal, align with, bolster, enhance (when overdone).

### 3. Inflated importance

Don't pump things up with: plays a pivotal/crucial/vital role, serves as a testament to, underscores the importance of, marks a new era, reflects broader trends, evolving landscape.

### 4. Promotional hype

Remove ad-speak: boasts, vibrant, breathtaking, groundbreaking, in the heart of, rich heritage, commitment to excellence, must-see, showcases, exemplifies.

### 5. AI-specific fancy words

Kill these overused ones: delve into, intricate, tapestry (metaphorical), nuances, interplay, meticulous, garner, underscore, fostering, valuable insights, landscape (abstract).

### 6. Heavy introductory phrases

Don't start with: It is important to note that, It is worth mentioning, One must consider, It cannot be overstated, In today's digital age.

### 7. Fancy replacements for simple verbs

Skip: serves as, stands as, represents, embodies, exemplifies, features, maintains, offers. Just say 'is,' 'are,' 'has,' or 'we have.'

### 8. Negative parallelisms

Use at most once per text: 'not only X but also Y,' 'not just X, but Y,' 'it's not about X, it's about Y.'

### 9. Rule of three addiction

Stop forcing everything into threes. Two or four items? Leave them as two or four.

### 10. Trailing participial fluff

Cut endings like: highlighting, underscoring, emphasizing, fostering, contributing to, ensuring, reflecting, shaping.

### 11. Abstract framing

Avoid: in the realm of, in the world of, in the evolving landscape of, creative space, arena, focal point.

### 12. Overused transitions

Drop repetitive starters: Additionally, Furthermore, Moreover, In addition, It is also worth noting, Building on this.

### 13. Unnecessary synonym swapping

If a word works, repeat it. Don't rotate hero to protagonist to central character just to sound varied.

### 14. Vague authority appeals

Skip: experts argue, observers note, industry reports suggest, several sources indicate. Name a real source or drop it.

### 15. Challenges-and-prospects template

Never drop in: 'Despite the challenges… continues to thrive / shows great promise.'

### 16. Generic upbeat endings

Avoid: The future looks bright, exciting times lie ahead, this is an important step forward, paving the way for a promising tomorrow.

### 17. Heavy hedging

Say it straight. Turn 'it can be argued that this potentially might have a certain impact' into 'this probably affects' or just state it.

### 18. Common wordy fillers

'in order to' → 'to' | 'the fact that' → cut | 'at the present time' → 'now' | 'in the event that' → 'if' | 'has the ability to' → 'can'

### 19. Chatbot habits and brown-nosing

Remove: 'I hope this helps,' 'Great question!', 'Absolutely!', 'You're absolutely right,' 'Let me know if you need anything else.'

### 20. Quote and punctuation quirks

Stick to proper curly quotes. Don't mix in straight quotes or overuse them for emphasis.

### 21. Decorative emojis and formatting

Don't slap emojis at the start of headings or list items just for looks. Skip random bolding unless it actually helps.

### 22. Too-perfect rhythm and missing personality

Watch for even sentence lengths, uniform paragraphs, no contractions, zero asides, and zero real opinion.

## How to Apply the Skill

1. Read it out loud if you can
2. Flag every issue from the list
3. Rewrite the bad parts
4. Check: flows naturally when spoken, sentences vary in length, concrete details replace vague stuff, original tone remains
5. Final gut check: 'What else gives it away?'
6. Tweak again if needed

## Adding Personality (This Part Matters Most)

Just removing the bad stuff isn't enough — clean but soulless text still feels fake. Push in some real life:

- Actual opinions and reactions, not neutral lists
- Mix short punchy sentences with longer ones
- Use 'I,' 'me,' or 'us' where it fits
- Honest mixed feelings ('this is powerful, but also kind of scary')
- Little bits of chaos — asides, 'honestly,' 'look,' 'the thing is'
- Specific images instead of blurry feelings

## Output Format

1. Clean final version after humanization
2. Short note on what changed (only if it helps)
